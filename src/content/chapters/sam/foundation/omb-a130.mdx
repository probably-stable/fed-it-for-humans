If FISMA is the constitution, A-130 is the bylaws. FISMA says federal agencies must have an information security program; A-130 tells them what kind, with what cadence, covering what, and points them at NIST for the technical specifics. It's Office of Management and Budget (OMB) policy, not law, but for federal agencies the distinction is academic. A-130 is binding.

<YouPanel persona="sam">
You probably won't read A-130. What you'll see is your agency's internal policy that derives from it, that's what your SSP will cite, what your ISSO will reference, what your training will refer to. A-130 itself stays in the background.

The piece of A-130 that may reach you most directly is the annual FISMA reporting cycle. A-130 specifies what kinds of reports federal agencies must produce, on what cadence, covering what. If your team owns any system, you'll likely be asked for inputs to those reports.

**First-week move:** ask what your agency's "policy" document is for information security. Whatever it's called, it's A-130 translated into your agency's voice, and that's the document you'll actually use.
</YouPanel>
