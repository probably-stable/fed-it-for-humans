If you take a job in federal IT, you will spend most of your career somewhere inside the RMF. It's the lifecycle that takes a system from "we want to build this" to "this system is authorized to operate" to "we're monitoring it forever." Every artifact you'll touch, the SSP, the POA&M, the security categorization, the controls assessment, the ATO letter, is produced during one of the seven steps below.

<YouPanel persona="sam">
This is your job. Whatever you do day-to-day in fed IT, it lives somewhere in this seven-step loop. You'll spend most of your time in **Implement** (configuring systems, applying STIGs, fixing scan findings) and **Monitor** (continuous scans, change management, POA&M maintenance). The assessors come around periodically. The AO signs once every 1-3 years. Everything else is you and your team executing.

The artifact you'll spend the most time inside is the **SSP**. That's the master document that describes your system and how it satisfies each control. When something changes, a config update, a new component, a finding, the SSP needs to reflect it.

**First-week move:** ask where your system's SSP lives and ask to read it. Reading one full SSP teaches you more about how RMF works in practice than any chapter on RMF can.
</YouPanel>
