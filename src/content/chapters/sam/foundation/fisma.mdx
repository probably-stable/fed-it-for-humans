Most people in federal IT first hear "FISMA" in a meeting and nod along because the alternative is admitting they don't know what it is. So let's just say it. FISMA is a law. It's the law that requires federal agencies to have an information security program. Everything you'll learn after this (NIST publications, agency policies, STIGs, the whole compliance apparatus) exists because FISMA exists.

<FismaCore />

<YouPanel persona="sam">
You'll see FISMA cited in your agency's policy documents, in the DFARS or FAR clauses on your contract, and in the boilerplate at the front of your System Security Plan (SSP). You'll never directly *implement* FISMA. You'll implement all the things downstream of it. The day-to-day pressure you feel (controls assessments, scan remediations, ATO maintenance) is FISMA-derived but reaches you through NIST 800-53 and your agency's overlay.

**First-week move:** ask your boss or ISSO what your team's role is in the agency's annual FISMA reporting cycle. The answer tells you which artifacts you'll be asked to produce when the deadline hits.

A reality check before we move on: most people in federal IT don't talk about FISMA day-to-day. You might hear it once from a senior or a mentor — usually framed as "FISMA is the law, everything else derives from it" — and then it sits as background. The work you'll actually feel is downstream: NIST 800-53 controls, ATOs, POA&Ms, scan findings. If you go your first six months in a fed IT job and never hear "FISMA" again after orientation, nobody would be surprised.
</YouPanel>
