A-130 hands the technical baton to NIST and walks off. Everything below A-130 in the chain — the actual standards, the control catalogs, the assessment procedures, the categorization frameworks — comes from NIST. If you do federal IT work, you will see "800-53" and "FIPS 199" and "RMF" cited in your contract, your SSP, your training, your audit findings. They're all NIST.

You'll probably read pieces of SP 800-53 and SP 800-37 over time, never end-to-end. The catalog is reference material — you look up specific controls when you're working on something or when an assessor flags a finding. Your day-to-day work happens via STIGs and CIS Benchmarks at the technical layer; the NIST docs are the WHY behind what STIGs ask you to do.

The most useful piece of the NIST catalog to know early is the relationship between FIPS 199 and SP 800-53B. FIPS 199 sorts your system into Low, Moderate, or High; SP 800-53B says which controls apply at each level. If you understand that mapping, half the questions about "why do we need this control" answer themselves.

**First-week move:** ask what your system's FIPS 199 categorization is. If your boss or ISSO can tell you "Moderate" or "High" without looking it up, you have a useful anchor for everything else.

If your work touches lab equipment, specialized research infrastructure, or any "computers controlling physical things," **SP 800-82 (OT security)** comes into play and you'll see it referenced in your agency's policies. Similarly, if your shop is rolling out Zero Trust per M-22-09, **SP 800-207** is the technical reference behind that effort.

## What's next

The next chapter goes one level deeper into the most-cited publication of the bunch: **NIST SP 800-37, the Risk Management Framework (RMF)**. This is the seven-step lifecycle federal agencies use to authorize and continuously monitor information systems. Almost every job posting in fed IT mentions it. Worth a chapter on its own.
