You'll never be assessed against 800-53. But it's the most comprehensive free public cybersecurity controls catalog in existence, and if you're building a security program from scratch and don't want to pay for ISO 27001 or wait through SOC 2 maturation, it's a solid reference. The Moderate baseline gives you a defensible "we cover the same ground a federal agency at moderate impact would" foundation. Easier cousin: NIST CSF, which references 800-53 underneath but presents it in a more accessible shape.

<YouPanel persona="tom">
800-53 doesn't apply to you legally. But it's the most comprehensive, free, publicly-maintained cybersecurity controls catalog in existence.

If you're building out a security program from scratch and don't want to pay for ISO 27001 or wait through SOC 2 maturation, 800-53 is a solid reference. The Moderate baseline gives you a defensible "we cover the same ground a federal agency at moderate impact would" foundation. Cheaper alternative: NIST CSF, which references 800-53 underneath but presents it in a more accessible shape.

You won't ever be assessed against 800-53. But the controls themselves are battle-tested at scale, and the document is free.
</YouPanel>

## What's next

Doesn't apply to you directly, but the concept ("start with a generic framework, tailor for your context") is what mature private-sector programs do too. Industry-specific frameworks like PCI-DSS and HITRUST are essentially private-sector "overlays."
