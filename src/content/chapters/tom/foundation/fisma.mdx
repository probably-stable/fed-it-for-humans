FISMA is federal law for federal agencies. You're not one, and most of your vendors aren't either. The law doesn't apply to you. But it shapes your world anyway, quietly, through vendor pricing, court citations, and state laws that copy federal patterns. Worth knowing what it is so the rest of the federal compliance vocabulary (which leaks into private-sector security thinking more than people realize) makes sense as you encounter it.

<YouPanel persona="tom">
FISMA doesn't apply to you. You're in the private sector, not handling federal data, not a federal contractor. The law is irrelevant to your day job.

But it shapes your world anyway, in three quiet ways:

1. Your vendors who serve federal customers (any FedRAMP'd SaaS, any tool that touts NIST compliance) carry FISMA-driven costs in their pricing, and pass them to you.
2. NIST cybersecurity standards (NIST CSF, NIST 800-53) get cited in court when "reasonable cybersecurity" comes up in negligence cases. Your liability picture is partly drawn by federal references.
3. State laws sometimes copy federal patterns: breach notification regimes, state cybersecurity statutes for regulated industries.

You don't have to do anything about FISMA. Just know that "the federal world" is closer to your world than it looks.
</YouPanel>
