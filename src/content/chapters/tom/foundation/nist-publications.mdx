None of the NIST publications apply to you legally — they're federal standards for federal use. What's useful to know: NIST publishes a lot of this work as free, high-quality public references, and a few have crossed into private-sector practice as voluntary frameworks. NIST CSF is the biggest one. Cyber insurers ask about CSF alignment. Court cases reference NIST when "reasonable cybersecurity" comes up. Worth knowing what's on the shelf.

NIST has free, high-quality publications you can use even though none apply to you legally.

The most useful one for private-sector security teams is **NIST CSF (Cybersecurity Framework)**. Six functions as of CSF 2.0, around 100 categories and subcategories, a maturity model, and tooling support across most major security platforms. Cybersecurity insurers are increasingly asking about CSF alignment in their questionnaires.

NIST also publishes hardening references for many products (often as Special Publications or via the National Checklist Program). All free. All public. Generally better quality than most paid alternatives.

You don't need any of this. But if you want a free, federally maintained, court-cited cybersecurity framework, NIST CSF is the door.
