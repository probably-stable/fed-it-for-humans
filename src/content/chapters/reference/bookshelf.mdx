This guide is the on-ramp. The official documents are where you go when you need the actual answer to a specific question. They're often denser than this guide, sometimes drier, occasionally near-unreadable end-to-end — but they're the ground truth.

For each major framework or document, here's where to find it, what it costs, and what it's actually like to read.

---

## Federal core (the foundation chain)

**FISMA (the law)**
*Codified at 44 U.S.C. § 3551 et seq. Search "FISMA 2014" or "44 USC 3551" — the law text is publicly available via Cornell's Legal Information Institute (law.cornell.edu) and the U.S. Code (uscode.house.gov).*
Free. Dense legalese; not the read you actually want unless you specifically need the statutory text. Wikipedia's FISMA article is the better practical entry point.

**OMB Circular A-130**
*whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/legacy_drupal_files/omb/circulars/A130/a130revised.pdf (or search "OMB Circular A-130 PDF").*
Free. Government-policy writing — readable but not gripping. The 2016 revision is the current version.

**NIST publications (FIPS + SP 800 series)**
*csrc.nist.gov — the canonical source. Browse the publication list, download PDFs free.*
Free, all of it. Quality varies — some publications are well-written (SP 800-37 RMF is reasonable), some are reference material you skim (SP 800-53 catalog). Use as lookup, not end-to-end reading.

Specific publications worth knowing by number:
- **FIPS 199** — categorization. Short. Worth reading once.
- **FIPS 200** — minimum requirements. Short. Worth reading once.
- **SP 800-37** — RMF. Read the executive summary and Chapter 1; skim the rest.
- **SP 800-53 rev 5** — controls catalog. Reference only. Look up controls when needed.
- **SP 800-53A** — assessment procedures. Reference for assessors.
- **SP 800-53B** — control baselines. Quick lookup.
- **SP 800-171** — CUI for non-federal systems. Defense-contractor-relevant.
- **SP 800-172** — enhanced CUI controls. Advanced threat picture.

**NIST CSF 2.0**
*nist.gov/cyberframework. The framework PDF is about 30 pages of actually readable content — the most readable thing NIST publishes.*
Free. The friendliest on-ramp to NIST thinking. Worth a read for anyone in security regardless of fed status.

## Specialized federal frameworks

**NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust Architecture)**
*csrc.nist.gov/publications/detail/sp/800-207/final.*
Free. Reasonable to read end-to-end if you're working on ZTA.

**NIST SP 800-82 (OT Security)**
*csrc.nist.gov — search "SP 800-82 rev 3."*
Free. Long but well-organized. Reference material for OT-specific work.

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework**
*nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework.*
Free. AI RMF 1.0 + companion AI 600-1 (Generative AI profile) both available. Newer territory; the framework itself is reasonable to read.

**OMB Memorandum M-22-09 (Federal Zero Trust Strategy)**
*whitehouse.gov — search "M-22-09."*
Free. Short policy memo. Worth a read if you're working on ZTA in a federal context.

## DoD-side documents (named for orientation; this guide doesn't cover defense contractor in depth)

**DoD Instructions** (DoDI 8500.01, DoDI 8510.01, etc.)
*esd.whs.mil/DD/issuances/dodi (DoD Issuances repository).*
Free. DoD writing — formal, structured, sometimes opaque to outsiders.

**DISA STIGs**
*public.cyber.mil — the DISA STIG Library. Free downloads in XCCDF format + the DISA STIG Viewer (also free).*
Free. Per-product hardening checklists. Reference material.

**DISA SRGs (Security Requirements Guides)**
*Same site as STIGs, in the SRG library.*
Free. The product-class-level overlay between NIST controls and product-specific STIGs.

## FedRAMP

**FedRAMP PMO documents**
*fedramp.gov — the program's official site. Templates, guidance, the FedRAMP Marketplace, training materials.*
Free. The PMO publishes their own SSP templates, control baselines, and continuous monitoring guidance. The training materials (free, on-site) are the best official on-ramp.

**FedRAMP Marketplace**
*marketplace.fedramp.gov — public list of authorized cloud services.*
Free. Useful for vendor evaluation regardless of whether you're federal yourself.

## Industry standards

**CIS Controls + CIS Benchmarks**
*cisecurity.org — both available as free PDFs (registration required for some). CIS-CAT scanner is free for non-commercial; commercial requires paid membership.*
Free for the documents. The CIS Controls v8 PDF is short and readable; the per-product Benchmarks are reference material.

**NIST CSF 2.0**
*nist.gov/cyberframework. (Same as above; included here for cross-reference.)*

**ISO 27001 / 27002**
*iso.org/standard/27001 and iso.org/standard/27002.*
**Paid.** ISO standards aren't free — purchase per copy from ISO directly or from national standards bodies (ANSI in the US). Costs roughly $100-200 USD per standard. Many large orgs buy company-wide licenses; consultants and certification bodies often have them.

**SOC 2 (AICPA Trust Services Criteria)**
*aicpa-cima.com — the AICPA's site. The full Trust Services Criteria PDF requires AICPA membership for free access; otherwise paid.*
Mixed. The high-level overview is free; the detailed criteria are gated.

**PCI-DSS v4.0.1**
*pcisecuritystandards.org — the PCI Security Standards Council's site. The standard itself is free; implementation guides and supporting documents also free.*
Free. The standard PDF is dense but well-organized. Currently version 4.0.

**HITRUST CSF**
*hitrustalliance.net.*
**Paid.** Full HITRUST CSF requires a subscription. Free overview + framework summary; the detailed control specifications are subscriber-only.

## Healthcare

**HIPAA Security Rule and Privacy Rule**
*hhs.gov/hipaa — the official HHS resource. Includes the rule text, official guidance, FAQs, and enforcement-action summaries.*
Free. The rule text is regulation language; HHS's own summaries and FAQs are more readable.

**HHS Security Risk Assessment (SRA) Tool**
*healthit.gov/topic/privacy-security-and-hipaa/security-risk-assessment-tool.*
Free. The tool HHS provides for HIPAA risk analyses. Useful structure even if you don't formally use it.

**FDA Pre-Market Cybersecurity Guidance for Medical Devices**
*fda.gov — search "premarket cybersecurity guidance."*
Free. The reference document for medical-device cybersecurity. Cross-references NIST SP 800-82.

## Privacy laws

**GDPR**
*gdpr-info.eu — clean unofficial version of the regulation. The EU's official version is at eur-lex.europa.eu.*
Free. Long but clearly structured (numbered articles, recitals separated). Articles 5-7 cover the principles you'll cite most often.

**State privacy laws (CCPA/CPRA, VCDPA, CPA, etc.)**
*Each state attorney general's office hosts the law. California's CCPA/CPRA is at oag.ca.gov/privacy/ccpa.*
Free. State-level analogs to GDPR; each has its own scope and enforcement.

## Federal acquisition (DFARS, FAR)

**FAR (Federal Acquisition Regulation)**
*acquisition.gov — the canonical source.*
Free. Massive. Almost nobody reads it end-to-end; you look up specific clauses when they appear in your contract.

**DFARS (Defense FAR Supplement)**
*Same site, DFARS section.*
Free. The DoD-specific extensions. DFARS clause 252.204-7012 is the one that pulls defense contractors into NIST 800-171.

## Certifications and study resources

These aren't compliance frameworks but they're where most people develop fluency in this material.

**(ISC)² CISSP** — broadest security certification. Books and study guides easily available. Plan for 6+ months of study.

**ISACA CISA** — audit-focused certification. Common in federal compliance roles.

**ISACA CISM** — security management certification.

**(ISC)² HCISPP** — healthcare-focused.

**IAPP CIPP/E and CIPP/US** — privacy law certifications. Useful for GDPR and U.S. state privacy work.

**ISO 27001 Lead Auditor** — accredited training programs from various bodies. If you want to do certification audits.

**FedRAMP PMO training** — fedramp.gov publishes free training materials. Best on-ramp for FedRAMP-specific roles.

**NIST CSF training** — NIST publishes free webinars and training materials at nist.gov/cyberframework/online-learning.

## Notes on reading

Most official docs in this space were written by committees, lawyers, or technical-standards authors. They're rarely page-turners. A few practical reading tips:

- **Start with the table of contents.** Read it once. Notice the structure. That alone tells you what the document is about and where to look.
- **Read the executive summary, then skip to specific sections you need.** End-to-end reading is rarely the right move.
- **Cross-reference between documents.** Most NIST docs reference other NIST docs; following the references gives you better context than reading any single doc alone.
- **Don't memorize.** Look up. The reference muscle is more valuable than the recall muscle.
