FISMA is federal. Your hospital isn't a federal agency, so the law doesn't bind you. But it shows up at your edges in two real ways: anywhere your hospital touches federal data flows (Medicare claims integrations, federally funded research data, VA telehealth, NIH grant systems), and as a shape that rhymes with the HIPAA Security Rule (both descend from late-1990s NIST and OMB risk-based security thinking, so they share a structural pedigree). Worth knowing what FISMA is before we look at how it reaches you.

<YouPanel persona="jordan">
FISMA doesn't apply to you in the federal sense: you're not a federal agency or contractor handling federal information systems. But two things make it worth knowing.

First, the HIPAA Security Rule and FISMA share a common architectural pedigree. Both descend from late-1990s NIST and OMB risk-based security thinking, so their shapes rhyme. Risk-based, document everything, designated security officer, periodic assessment. The vocabulary differs but the muscle is similar.

Second, if any of your hospital's systems touch federal data (Medicare or Medicaid claims integrations, federally funded research data, VA telehealth, NIH grant systems), that *specific* system can be in FISMA scope while the rest of your hospital isn't.

**First-week move:** ask your security officer or compliance lead whether any of your systems handle federal data flows. The answer changes which compliance regime governs that system.
</YouPanel>

## What's next

The next link in the chain is **OMB Circular A-130**, the policy document that operationalizes FISMA's high-level requirements into specific obligations for federal agencies. If FISMA is the law, A-130 is the implementing policy. It doesn't apply to you either, but its architectural shape has direct HIPAA-side analogs worth recognizing.
