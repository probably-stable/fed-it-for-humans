## Impact levels

FedRAMP uses four impact levels, mapped from FIPS 199 categorization with FedRAMP-specific control baselines:

- **Low**, low-impact systems. Roughly 125 controls.
- **Li-SaaS**, Low-impact SaaS. A lighter variant of Low introduced in 2017 for collaboration / productivity SaaS that doesn't store significant federal data. Reduced control set.
- **Moderate**, the most common authorization level for federal SaaS. Roughly 325 controls.
- **High**, sensitive federal data. Roughly 410+ controls. Most stringent.

The level you're authorized at determines the size of your control surface and, downstream, the cost and time of your authorization and continuous monitoring effort.

## Authorization paths

Two paths historically existed. The landscape is in active modernization (see the In-the-field note below), but the structural distinction is worth knowing:

- **Agency ATO.** A single sponsoring agency's AO authorizes your cloud service for federal use. Other agencies can then leverage that authorization for their own ATOs (with their own additional overlay if they want one). This is the more common path.
- **JAB Provisional ATO (P-ATO).** The Joint Authorization Board (DHS + DoD + GSA CIOs, historically) issues a provisional authorization that any federal agency can leverage without a single sponsor. Higher bar to enter; broader reach when achieved.

## The cast

- **CSP (Cloud Service Provider).** The company offering the service.
- **3PAO (Third-Party Assessment Organization).** FedRAMP-accredited assessor. Conducts the security assessment using SP 800-53A procedures against the FedRAMP baseline.
- **AO (Authorizing Official).** At the sponsoring agency for Agency path, or on the JAB for JAB path.
- **FedRAMP PMO.** The GSA team that runs the program, maintains the marketplace, manages the JAB process, accredits 3PAOs.
- **Federal customer agency.** The buyer that ultimately uses (and sometimes sponsors) the authorization.

## Continuous monitoring

Authorization isn't the end; it's the start. Once authorized, a CSP runs continuous monitoring: monthly POA&M updates, vulnerability scans on a defined cadence, change management for system modifications, annual reassessment of a subset of controls, and full reassessment on a longer cycle. The 3PAO stays engaged for the ongoing assessment work. The federal customer agencies expect continuous monitoring deliverables on a regular schedule.

This is the part that surprises new CSPs the most: the cost and operational burden of FedRAMP isn't the initial authorization. It's the multi-year continuous monitoring obligation that follows.

<InTheField>
FedRAMP has been in active modernization for several years (sometimes branded "FedRAMP 20x" or under similar names depending on the cycle). The JAB P-ATO process was being restructured and partially phased out in favor of a streamlined Agency-path authorization with broader reusability. Authorization timelines that historically ran 12-24 months are explicitly being targeted for reduction. The structural shape (tailored 800-53 baseline, 3PAO assessment, AO authorization, continuous monitoring) is stable. The specific procedural state (which path is currently preferred, how much process automation has landed, what the JAB's role is) drifts. Verify against current FedRAMP PMO guidance at fedramp.gov before relying on procedural specifics.
