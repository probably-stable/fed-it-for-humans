## The major agency overlay families

A short, non-exhaustive tour of overlay families you'll encounter or hear about. Each cabinet department has its own; the named examples below are the ones with the most public-facing documentation.

- **DoD, DoDI 8500.01 (Cybersecurity) + DoDI 8510.01 (RMF for DoD IT) + DISA SRGs.** The DoD operates its own RMF instance with overlay layers stacked: department-wide instructions at 8500-series, RMF process at 8510, and DISA Security Requirements Guides (SRGs) as the cross-product overlay before STIGs. Individual services (Army, Navy, Air Force, Space Force) layer service-specific guidance on top.

- **Intelligence Community, CNSSI 1253 + ICD 503 + JSIG.** The IC uses CNSSI 1253 (Committee on National Security Systems Instruction) for categorization of national security systems, with different impact dimensions than FIPS 199. ICD 503 (Intelligence Community Directive) handles IT system security risk management. JSIG (Joint SAP Implementation Guide) covers Special Access Programs. Cleared environments only.

- **DOE, DOE Order 205.1 series (Department Cyber Security Program).** Department of Energy overlay; carries additional weight where work touches nuclear-related sensitivity.

- **HHS, HHS Cybersecurity Program.** Health and Human Services has its own info-sec program, on top of the HIPAA framework HHS itself maintains for the broader healthcare sector.

- **Other cabinet agencies (VA, State, Treasury, civilian science and research agencies, etc.).** Every cabinet department has its own overlay. Some are well-known to specialists; most are internal documents you wouldn't read until you worked there. The document type varies by agency (Orders, Instructions, Directives, Procedural Requirements), depending on the agency's own conventions.

The pattern is consistent: NIST 800-53 baseline, then agency overlay, then system implementation. The overlay is where the agency's mission, culture, and risk tolerance get encoded into the controls.

<InTheField>
Agency overlay maturity and accessibility vary widely. Some agencies have well-organized overlay catalogs you can pull up from a single internal portal: controls cross-walked to NIST, agency-specific implementation guidance attached to each, lineage clear. Others have the overlay scattered across multiple documents across multiple offices, with center-level or service-level guidance on top, and no single canonical map. If you're walking into a new shop, ask where the agency's overlay lives and how to read it. The answer is sometimes one URL and sometimes a SharePoint scavenger hunt.
