The seven steps, plain language:

1. **Prepare** — organizational and system-level groundwork before the lifecycle starts. Identifies stakeholders, common controls, risk tolerance. Added in rev 2 (2018) to formalize what good agencies were already doing.
2. **Categorize** — apply FIPS 199 to determine the system's impact level (Low, Moderate, High) based on confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
3. **Select** — pick the appropriate controls from SP 800-53, using the SP 800-53B baseline that matches the FIPS 199 category. Tailor as needed.
4. **Implement** — actually deploy the controls. This is where STIGs, CIS Benchmarks, and vendor-specific hardening guides get applied.
5. **Assess** — an independent assessor evaluates whether the controls are working as documented. Findings get recorded.
6. **Authorize** — the Authorizing Official reviews everything, weighs the risk, and either grants ATO (Authorization to Operate), grants a conditional or interim ATO, or denies. This is the major milestone.
7. **Monitor** — continuous monitoring kicks in. Ongoing scans, periodic re-assessment, change management, POA&M tracking. The system stays in this step until it's decommissioned.

The whole loop produces three artifacts you'll see referenced constantly: the **System Security Plan (SSP)**, the **Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M)**, and the **ATO letter** itself.

<InTheField>
How long an authorized system stays authorized varies by shop. Some agencies still do **fixed-period reauthorization** — three-year cycles where the system goes through a full re-assessment to renew its ATO. Others have moved to **ongoing authorization** — the system stays authorized as long as continuous-monitoring metrics stay green, with no fixed expiration. The model your shop runs determines whether you experience compliance work as a periodic scramble or a steady background load. Worth asking which one your agency uses.
