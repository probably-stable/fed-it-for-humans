## The five forks

A short orientation. Each fork is named below with what it covers; the dedicated chapters that follow go deeper on the ones this guide covers.

- **Federal direct**, the foundation chain by itself. You work for or with a federal agency (civilian or DoD) on federal information systems, and the chain we just walked is your whole picture. No additional fork; this is the baseline. Covered in the foundation set.

- **Defense contractor (CMMC + 800-171 + DFARS)**, **out of scope for this guide.** If you handle Controlled Unclassified Information for the DoD or its supply chain *in your own (non-federal) environment*, you're on this fork, CMMC certification, NIST SP 800-171, DFARS clause 252.204-7012, and a program landscape that's been actively reshaping for years. The author of this guide doesn't have direct lived experience with this fork and won't write speculatively about it. If you're on it, the official sources (the CMMC Accreditation Body, DoD CIO, NIST 800-171 itself) and practitioners who do this work day-to-day are where to go. *Open invitation: if a contributor with direct CMMC / 800-171 / DFARS experience wants to write this chapter, the project would welcome it.*

- **Classified**, additional overlay layers on top of NIST baselines. CNSSI 1253 for categorization (different impact dimensions than FIPS 199), ICD 503 for IC IT system risk management, JSIG for Special Access Programs. Cleared environments only. Named here for orientation; depth coverage is limited in this guide because the operational reality of cleared work is something the author hasn't lived. The framework names and authority are accurate; the day-to-day shape is best learned from people who do it.

- **Cloud services (FedRAMP)**, Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program. A standardized way for cloud services to be authorized once and reused across federal agencies. Built on a tailored NIST 800-53 baseline, assessed by FedRAMP-accredited 3PAOs, authorized by either Agency AOs or the JAB. If you provide a cloud service to federal customers, this is your fork. Dedicated chapter forthcoming.

- **Industry standards**, CIS Controls + NIST CSF + industry-specific (PCI-DSS for payments, HIPAA + HITRUST for healthcare, SOC 2 / ISO 27001 for service providers, GDPR / state privacy laws for data handlers). Not governed by federal compliance regimes; runs alongside. Many private-sector orgs voluntarily adopt federal frameworks (NIST CSF most commonly) without being required to. Dedicated chapter forthcoming.

## How to know which fork(s) apply to you

Some quick triage questions:

- **Do you have a federal contract?** If yes, you're on the federal direct fork at minimum. If the contract involves cloud services, add FedRAMP. If the work touches classified information, add the classified fork.
- **Is the data classified?** If yes, the classified fork applies regardless of what other forks are also in play.
- **Do you handle CUI for DoD in your own (non-federal) environment?** If yes, defense contractor fork (CMMC + 800-171 + DFARS), out of scope for this guide; see official CMMC / NIST / DoD CIO references.
- **Are you a cloud service used by federal agencies?** If yes, FedRAMP fork.
- **Are you in the private sector with no federal contracts?** Industry standards fork. Industry-specific within that (PCI, HIPAA, SOC 2, etc.) depending on what you handle.

A single system frequently lands on multiple forks at once. A defense contractor running a cloud service handling CUI is on three forks: federal direct (baseline), defense contractor (CMMC, out of scope here), and FedRAMP (cloud). All three apply; all three need to be satisfied; only some are covered in this guide.

<InTheField>
The fork landscape is not static. CMMC's program rules have been actively reshaping over the last few years, assessment cadence, third-party assessor accreditation, what level of CMMC applies to which contract type, and the timeline for full rollout have all moved more than once. FedRAMP is in its own modernization push (the FedRAMP authorization process has been re-architected to shorten timelines and reduce cost). Whatever any chapter or guide says about the specific procedural state of a fork, verify against the current program documentation before relying on it. The structural shape stays stable; the specifics drift.
